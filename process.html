Process Conditions & Technology

How temperature, pressure, catalysts, and separation systems control naphtha reforming

Reaction Conditions

Operating Temperature

Typical reactor temperatures are 500–540°C. Higher temperatures favor endothermic reactions such as dehydrogenation and dehydrocyclization, which increase octane number and form aromatics.

Pressure Conditions

Modern reformers operate at < 7 bar. Lower pressure favors reactions that produce more gas (hydrogen) and helps reduce coke formation on the catalyst surface.

Hydrogen Atmosphere

The process runs in a hydrogen-rich environment, typically a ~5:1 hydrogen-to-hydrocarbon ratio. Hydrogen suppresses unstable unsaturated compounds and slows catalyst coking.

Bifunctional Catalyst System

Reforming catalysts are bifunctional, meaning they have two types of active sites: a metallic function (usually platinum, sometimes rhenium, chlorine, or fluorine) for hydrogenation/dehydrogenation, and an acidic support (commonly alumina) that promotes isomerization and cyclization. The balance between the two functions strongly affects performance.

Feedstock Preparation

The feedstock is a petroleum fraction called naphtha, usually obtained from atmospheric distillation of crude oil.

Naphtha Source

Naphtha typically boils between 30°C and 200°C and contains hydrocarbons with about 5 to 12 carbon atoms. This mixture contains paraffins, naphthenes, and other hydrocarbons.

Hydrotreating (Pre-treatment)

Before entering the reformer, naphtha is hydrotreated to remove impurities such as sulfur, nitrogen, and metals. These impurities can poison the catalyst, reducing activity and lifetime.

Why Hydrotreating Is Essential

Removing catalyst poisons improves stability, reduces deactivation, and helps keep reforming reactions selective toward high-octane reformate and aromatics.

Product Separation & Purification

After the reactors, the mixture is cooled and separated into gas and liquid streams to recover hydrogen and produce stabilized reformate.

Hydrogen Recovery

  • Hot product stream is cooled and sent to a separator.
  • Hydrogen gas is removed and compressed.
  • Most hydrogen is recycled back to maintain hydrogen-rich conditions.
  • Excess hydrogen becomes a valuable byproduct

Reformate Stabilization

  • The liquid product is sent to a fractionating column (stabilizer).
  • Light hydrocarbons (gases) are removed by distillation.
  • The final product is high-octane reformate.
  • Reformate can be blended into gasoline or processed to extract aromatics.

Octane Number vs. Aromatic Content

A main goal of reforming is increasing octane number. This is closely linked to higher aromatic content in the product, because aromatics generally have higher octane ratings than straight-chain paraffins.
