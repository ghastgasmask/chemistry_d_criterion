Chemical Reactions

The Chemistry of Naphtha Reforming

1. Dehydrogenation of Naphthenes to Aromatics

Endothermic

This is a highly desirable reaction for increasing the octane number and producing aromatics. It is endothermic and produces hydrogen.

Example: Conversion of cyclohexane to benzene

C6H12 (cyclohexane) → C6H6 (benzene) + 3H2

Explanation:

Cyclohexane, a naphthene, loses three molecules of hydrogen to form benzene, an aromatic compound. This reaction significantly increases the octane rating of the fuel.

2. Isomerization of Paraffins

Slightly Exothermic

Straight-chain paraffins, which have low octane numbers, are converted into their branched-chain isomers, which possess higher octane numbers. This reaction is slightly exothermic.

Example: Isomerization of n-heptane to iso-heptane

n-C7H16 (n-heptane) ⇌ iso-C7H16 (iso-heptane)

Explanation:

The prefix "n-" in n-C7H16 denotes a normal or straight-chain alkane, meaning all seven carbon atoms are arranged in a continuous, unbranched line. Straight-chain paraffins like n-heptane typically have very low octane numbers (n-heptane itself has an octane rating of 0). In this reaction, the straight-chain n-heptane rearranges its molecular structure to form a branched isomer, iso-heptane, without changing its chemical formula. This structural change significantly improves its anti-knock properties and thus its octane rating.

3. Dehydrocyclization of Paraffins to Aromatics

Endothermic

This reaction converts straight-chain paraffins into cyclic compounds (naphthenes) and then further dehydrogenates them into aromatics. This is also an endothermic reaction that produces hydrogen.

Example: Dehydrocyclization of n-heptane to toluene

n-C7H16 (n-heptane) → C7H8 (toluene) + 4H2

Explanation:

A straight-chain paraffin, such as n-heptane, undergoes cyclization to form a ring structure, followed by dehydrogenation to yield an aromatic compound like toluene. This reaction is crucial for both octane enhancement and aromatic production.

4. Hydrocracking

Secondary Reaction

This reaction involves the breaking of larger hydrocarbon molecules into smaller ones in the presence of hydrogen. While it can produce lighter, more volatile components, excessive hydrocracking is generally undesirable as it can lead to a loss of valuable liquid products and consume hydrogen.

Example: Hydrocracking of a decane molecule

C10H22 (decane) + H2 → C5H12 (pentane) + C5H12 (pentane)

Explanation:

A larger paraffin molecule breaks down into two smaller paraffin molecules. While useful for reducing molecular size, it can decrease the overall yield of high-octane reformate if not controlled.
